\subparagraph{The variables used}
The following variables were selected for the analysis, on the basis of 
their theoretical relevance to the research question and their availability 
across the countries and time period considered.\\
\textbf{Gini Index} measures income inequality within a country, expressed 
as a percentage ranging from 0 (perfect equality) to 100 (maximum inequality). 
Although theoretically relevant, this variable presented significant 
missingness beyond 2022, which limited its use in the statistical model \cite{Gini}.\\
\textbf{Depression Rates} capture the prevalence of depressive disorders 
across countries. Despite not being complete for all observations, data 
were available up to 2023 for a sufficient number of countries, making 
the variable usable for the purposes of this analysis \cite{ihme_depression_vizhub}.\\
\textbf{GNI per Capita} represents the total income earned by a country's 
residents divided by the population. It was preferred over GDP as it 
accounts for income from overseas investments and foreign aid, while 
subtracting capital outflows \cite{investopedia_gni}, thus providing a 
more accurate measure of the actual purchasing power of a country's residents \cite{worldbank_gni_per_capita}.\\
\textbf{Internet Users}, expressed as a percentage of the total population, 
serves as a proxy for the degree of digitalization of a country. It is 
included under the assumption that more digitalized countries tend to 
exhibit higher video game sales \cite{worldbank_internet_users} (there were 2 missing observations for India which have been integrated with \cite{statista_internet_users_india}).\\
\textbf{Population} (expressed in millions) was included as an auxiliary 
variable, used to normalize total sales figures and obtain a per capita 
measure that allows for more meaningful cross-country comparisons \cite{worldbank_population_total}.\\
\textbf{Sales} (expressed in millions) constitute the response variable 
of this study and represent the total video game sales recorded in each 
country for each year considered \cite{factset_equity_data}.\\
\textbf{Sales Variations} measure the relative change in sales between 
two consecutive periods, computed as:
\[
\Delta S_t = \frac{S_t - S_{t-1}}{S_{t-1}}
\]
where $S_t$ denotes total sales at time $t$.\\
\textbf{Sales per Capita} were derived by dividing total sales by the 
country's population for the corresponding year:
\[
S_t^{pc} = \frac{S_t}{P_t}
\]
where $P_t$ denotes the population at time $t$. This transformation 
removes the mechanical effect of country size on aggregate sales figures.\\
\textbf{Stringency Index} was included to capture the intensity of 
COVID-19 containment policies. It ranges from 0 (no restrictions) to 
100 (most severe restrictions), and is derived from the Oxford 
COVID-19 Government Response Tracker \cite{hale2026oxcgrt}.\\
\textbf{Unemployment Rate}, expressed as a percentage of the inactive 
population, reflects labor market conditions. Its relationship with 
video game sales is theoretically ambiguous: higher unemployment may 
increase leisure time, but simultaneously reduces disposable income 
available for discretionary spending \cite{worldbank_unemployment_ilo}.


\subparagraph{Data Aggregation: Countries, Macro-regions and Continents}\\
The country-level data represent the original observations retrieved from 
the sources described above. To improve the interpretability of the 
graphical representations, two levels of aggregation were derived: 
macro-regions and continents.\\
Both aggregations were performed in RStudio following the same procedure, 
differing only in the grouping criterion applied. For non-linear variables, 
a weighted mean was computed across the countries within each group, using 
a custom implementation that excludes observations with missing values to 
ensure numerical stability. For linear variables (such as sales and 
population), the group-level value was obtained by summing the 
corresponding country-level figures.
More technical details can be checked by viewing the files in the repository.
\section{Cleaning criteria}
The first step of the data preparation process consisted in converting all raw ‘.csv’ files into ‘.xlsx’ format. Subsequently, the datasets were processed in R Studio through a series of filtering and transformation procedures aimed at selecting only the variables relevant to the research.
\\
For datasets updated on a daily basis, such as the Stringency Index dataset, annual averages were computed in order to obtain one observation per year. In contrast, for yearly-updated datasets containing only a single variable, the cleaning process simply required selecting the countries and years relevant for the analysis.
\\
To better describe the heterogeneity of the raw datasets, the files were classified into four different categories according to their degree of organization and usability, ranging from the most unstructured datasets to the fully cleaned and standardized ones. The objective of this classification was to progressively transform all datasets into a common structure suitable for the universal R scripts prepared for the analysis.
\\\\
Please note that the procedure described below represents a simplified overview of the data-cleaning process. Since the datasets were collected from multiple online sources, minor inconsistencies across files prevented the use of fully standardized automated scripts. In particular, some variables and labels differed slightly between datasets and therefore required preliminary manual harmonization. For example, certain datasets referred to “South Korea” as “Republic of Korea”, while others used different column headers such as “Country” instead of “Country Name”.\\
In these cases, the inconsistencies were manually corrected in Excel to ensure uniformity across datasets before proceeding with the automated cleaning and merging procedures.\\
Furthermore, it should be noted that the dataset containing the financial variables was independently constructed with the support of the FactSet Excel integration tool. A more detailed explanation of this process will be provided in the following sections.
\\\\
\textbf{Class IV – Highly Unstructured Datasets}\\
This category includes datasets that were initially difficult to read and process due to the presence of excessive daily observations and multiple unnecessary variables, such as the Stringency Index dataset.\\
For these datasets, the variable of interest was isolated and annual mean values were computed for the 2020–2022 period. This procedure allowed the removal of daily observations and irrelevant information not required for the research purposes.\\
After this transformation, the datasets could be converted into a more organized structure corresponding to \textbf{Class II}.
\\\\
\textbf{Class III – Large but Structured Datasets}\\
This class includes datasets that were already partially organized, such as those obtained from the World Bank database, but contained observations for a very large number of years/countries, outside the scope of the analysis.\\
In this case, the cleaning procedure consisted of selecting only the years relevant for the research period (2014–2024) for each country present in the files.\\
This step reduced the dimensionality of the datasets and transformed them into a format compatible with the subsequent processing stage.
\\\\
\textbf{Class II – Structured but Non-Standardized Datasets}\\
Datasets in this category were already well organized but still presented two main issues:

* they included countries not considered in the analysis;

* they were not arranged according to the desired final layout.\\
Therefore, specific R scripts were used to retain only the selected countries and standardize the structure of the datasets.\\
The final objective was to obtain a layout composed of three vertical columns:

* country (with observations listed in alphabetical order);

* year;

* value of the variable.
This structure facilitated the integration of all variables into the final dataset through copy-and-paste operations on Excel.\\
The chosen layout consisted of having one observation for each country-year combination (e.g., Italy 2014, Italy 2015, etc.) arranged vertically, while the variables were organized horizontally with their corresponding values disposed vertically.
\\\\
\textbf{Class I – Fully Cleaned and Standardized Datasets}\\
This final category includes datasets that were completely cleaned, standardized, and fully compatible with the requirements of the empirical analysis.\\
At this stage, all variables shared the same structure and layout, making the datasets directly usable for the final merging process and the analysis (they all had to have 430 rows corresponding to:\\ 1 (header) + [39 (countries) * 11 (years from 2014-2024) ] ).\\
The columns of values of datasets pertaining to the I class could simply be copied and pasted in the final/merged dataset.
\\\\
\textbf{How were financial data retrieved?}\\
Financial data collection represented the most complex part of the dataset construction, as it required defining the structure of the firms portfolio, estimating revenues, and allocating them across countries based on the average percentage distribution of sales within the entire portfolio.\\
A key challenge concerned the retrieval of comparable and consistent financial data. In particular, data for Microsoft and Sony were not directly obtainable from FactSet in a disaggregated form suitable for the analysis. These firms report revenues across multiple business segments beyond gaming; therefore, isolating only the gaming-related portion over a ten-year period was not feasible using standard screening tools.\\
To address this limitation, I extracted information from the official Form 10-K filings of both Microsoft and Sony for the entire period under analysis. For Microsoft, I focused on the “Xbox” segment, while for Sony I considered the “Game \& Network Services” division. This approach allowed for a more precise identification of gaming-specific revenues.\\
For Sony, revenues reported in Japanese Yen were converted into US dollars using annual exchange rates. To ensure consistency and comparability, exchange rates aligned with those applied in FactSet were adopted.\\
For the remaining firms, data were obtained through a combination of FactSet’s “Universal Screening” tool and additional firm-level verification. The screening process applied a 90\% threshold for gaming-related revenues, ensuring that only companies with a dominant exposure to the gaming industry were included.\\
Finally, firms that either did not meet the required level of industry focus or only recently shifted toward gaming-related activities were excluded. This step was supported by additional qualitative verification of company profiles to ensure consistency in classification over time.\\
From this dataset, I obtained firms’ net sales from 2014 to 2024. I then aggregated annual values to compute total revenue by year.\\
Subsequently, I applied the FMA\_GEOREV\_PCT metric to derive the percentage distribution of sales across countries for the dataset. Based on the average GEOREV share, I selected the countries exceeding a \~0.2\% threshold (on average), resulting in a final sample of 40 countries for the analysis. It is worth noting that, in the computation of country-level sales, Sony and Microsoft were also included, despite their diversified operations across multiple business segments. Nevertheless, this approach was preferred over excluding them, as doing so would have led to a disproportionate representation of Asia in the sample, largely driven by Nintendo’s regional concentration.\\
To obtain sales expressed in millions at the country level, I multiplied each country’s percentage share by the total net sales for each respective year.\\
Finally, in order to improve the readability of some graphical representations, I aggregated the country-level data into both macro-regional and continental groups.

\subsubsection{Depression Rates}
It is interesting to notice that, across all continents, there was an increase in depression rates starting from 2020, most likely driven by COVID-19 and the related lockdowns and restriction measures.\\
From our data, the only continent where a visible correlation between depression rates and video game sales emerges is Europe, particularly in Western Europe

\subsubsection{GNI per capita}
As we can see, the lowest GNI per capita is found in Africa, which is also the continent with the lowest sales among all regions.\\
In the Americas, there is a clear distinction between North and South America. While GNI per capita remains relatively stable in the southern part of the continent, the same cannot be said for the northern side, which shows a noticeable increase starting from 2020, with the USA reaching a GNI per capita up to five times higher than that of the southern region.\\
Regarding Asia, Singapore stands out from the rest of Southeast Asian countries, while Middle Eastern countries appear to be quite heterogeneous. In East Asia, China is the only country with a GNI per capita below the \$25,000 threshold.\\
With regard to Europe, while Eastern and Southern countries tend to fall within similar ranges, there are two countries that stand out from their respective regions: Switzerland in Western Europe and Norway in Northern Europe.

\subsubsection{Internet Users}
For Africa, Egypt's internet users' data were not available.
In general, what is surprising is noticing how there was not an upward trend in the COVID-19 period.\\
That said, Asia is the only continent which shows high variability between its countries having its countries spread in a range between the highest and the lowest end (India and Pakistan on the low end), while most of Southeast countries had a drastic increase in internet use percentage over the time.

\subsubsection{Log Sales In Millions}
As we could have expected, we notice NA's (North America's) levels being extremely high with respect to other regions. This is probably due to its higher market size and purchasing power simultaneously.\\
Remaining on the leading regions' side, we notice East Asia being the second group of countries with the highest number of sales, despite not being the Asian region with the highest GNI. As we can see from the sales per capita, this is partially (but not totally) due to the high population density of the latter.\\
Talking about Europe, it's interesting to see that despite it being one of the continents with highest GNI numbers, it doesn't contribute to the video games' market as much as one could expect in relation with the average income (even from the sales per capita, we see how it doesn't really distinguish itself from other continents.

\subsubsection{Sales per Capita}
Regarding sales per capita, we observe that in North America, the United States reaches the highest levels, as expected.\\
What is more interesting is that, although East Asia shows a significantly lower GNI per capita compared to Eastern Europe, its sales per capita used to be higher than Europe’s prior to 2021. After that point, however, we can see Europe’s levels increasing in line with its GNI growth. Even taking this into account, it still appears that the Asian population has a relatively higher propensity to spend on video games compared to the European population, when considering their respective GNI constraints.

\subsubsection{Unemployment}
The country that distinguishes itself the most from all the others is South Africa, reaching levels above 30\% since 2021 significantly exceeding the levels observed in all other countries included in the analysis.\\
Another outlier in the unemployment graph is Spain, which had very high unemployment levels at the beginning, which decreased over time (but always remaining above the European average).
Apart from these two outliers, all the other countries appear to be on the same level overall.\\

\subsection{Overall consideration about COVID-19's influence in sales}
From these data, several considerations can be made. On one hand, the graphs [\ref{fig:LOG_Colored_Unemployement_&_Sales_Graph}, \ref{fig:Internet Penetration and Log Sales}, \ref{fig:COVID-19 Restrictions and Log Sales}] suggest that structural economic factors may play a more important role than short-term shocks in explaining video game sales. In particular, while restrictions do not seem to show a clear relationship with sales, variables such as internet penetration and unemployment appear to be more strongly associated with them. This would imply that COVID-19 acted as a temporary shock, while development-related factors represent more persistent determinants of consumption patterns.\\
However, this interpretation is not entirely supported when considering the time series of sales of the different continents [\ref{fig:Cumulative Sales Indexed Graph}]. In this case, it becomes evident that COVID-19 did have a noticeable impact on sales levels. 
At this point, what we have to do is trying, as best as we can, to isolate the cause of that sales increase.
In fact, considering that in November 2020 the next-generation consoles from Microsoft and Sony (Xbox Series X and PlayStation 5) were released, without the help of some other data, it could be difficult to identify the exact cause of the sales increase.\\
Given the absence of a clear correlation between the stringency index and video game sales, the observed increase could be partially explained by the launch of these consoles, which are key players in the gaming hardware market.\\
A robust approach to understanding this increase would be to examine the evolution of video game and console sales during 2020 prior to November and see if it is coherent with the graph from Chapter 1 of Steam players increase in time spent (assuming that that increase would have regarded console gamers too and that an increase in time spent gaming leads to an increase in game industry sales).\\
